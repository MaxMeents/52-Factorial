The famous thought experiment

Can you even imagine a number this big?

Here is one of the most elegant analogies ever written — attributed to Scott Czepiel — to give you a sense of just how unfathomable 52! really is.

  1. 1

    Set a timer for eternity

    Pick your favorite spot on the equator. You are going to walk around the Earth — but take just one step every billion years.

    🌍 Between each step, play a round of solitaire with your deck.

  2. 2

    Drain the Pacific Ocean

    When you finally complete a full lap of the Earth, remove one drop of water from the Pacific Ocean.

    Then walk around the planet again. And again. Removing a single drop each lap — until the entire ocean is dry.

    💧 The Pacific Ocean contains about 700 quintillion drops.

  3. 3

    Stack paper to the Sun

    When the ocean is empty, lay one sheet of paper flat on the ground.

    Refill the ocean. Start the entire process over — circling the globe, emptying the ocean drop by drop — then add another sheet to your stack. Repeat until the paper reaches from the Earth to the Sun.

    📄 That's 93 million miles of paper.

  4. 4

    Play cards for a royal flush

    Now knock the paper stack over and start again. This time, every billion years shuffle your deck and deal yourself a five-card poker hand. If it isn't a royal flush, carry on.

    Every time you do get a royal flush, buy yourself a lottery ticket. If it wins, throw a single grain of sand into the Grand Canyon.

    🎰 Royal flush odds: 1 in 649,740.

  5. 5

    Fill the Grand Canyon

    Keep going — walking, draining, stacking, shuffling, winning lotteries — adding grains of sand one at a time until the Grand Canyon is completely full.

  6. 6

    Flatten a mountain

    Empty the canyon, and place a single ounce of rock on top of Mount Everest.

    Repeat the entire process — including refilling the canyon with grains of sand — each time removing another ounce from the mountain, until Everest has been ground down to sea level.

  7. ∞

    And you're still not there.

    At this point, look down at your deck. The number of arrangements you've worked through is… a rounding error. The seconds remaining before you've counted all 52! shuffles still exceed the age of the universe many times over.

So the next time you shuffle a deck…

Pause for a moment. The order of cards in your hands is, with overwhelming certainty, an arrangement no human, no machine, no being anywhere in the cosmos has ever seen before — or will ever see again.
